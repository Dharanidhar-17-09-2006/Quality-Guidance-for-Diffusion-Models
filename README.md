# Inference Guidance and LoRA Finetuning for Image Realness Improvement

This project investigates whether image realism can be improved by optimizing a learned **naturalness/rationality score** during both inference-time guidance and parameter-efficient finetuning (LoRA).

## 📌 Overview

The work explores the interaction between Stable Diffusion v1.5 and the **JOINT rationality scorer** (trained on the AGIN dataset). By analyzing the behavior of Low-Frequency Maps (LFM) and texture preservation, we highlight a critical divergence between automated metrics and human perception.

---

# Methodology

## 1. Inference-Time Rationality Guidance
The DDIM sampling loop was modified to apply gradient ascent on the rationality score during the final denoising steps.
* **Observation:** Rationality scores consistently improve (e.g., `3.22` → `3.29`).
* **Result:** No visible perceptual differences are observed.

## 2. Low-Frequency Map (LFM) Comparison
We investigated preprocessing methods to find the most useful gradients for guidance:
* **Gaussian Blur:** Scores images lower statically, but produces the most effective guidance gradients (+0.048 mean improvement).
* **AT Minimizer:** Changes images too aggressively, making gradients ineffective for latent guidance.

## 3. LoRA Finetuning & Fading Artifacts
We trained LoRA modules (Rank 16) by selecting the best-scored latents in a batch as a reward signal.
* **Failure Mode:** All models produced faded outputs and oversmoothed textures.
* **Diagnosis:** The `quality_R` scorer favors globally smooth images, teaching the model to "cheat" the metric by sacrificing high-frequency detail.

## 4. Proposed Fix: Texture Penalty
To counter the smoothing bias, a differentiable texture penalty was introduced:
`loss = mse_loss + 0.3 * ReLU(TARGET_TEXTURE - texture_energy(decoded_pred))`

* **Empirical Improvement:** Successfully preserved sharpness and reduced fading, creating perceptually superior images despite slightly lower numerical scores.

# Final Conclusion

This work investigates whether optimizing learned naturalness scores can improve the realism of images generated by Stable Diffusion.

Across all inference guidance experiments, rationality-based guidance consistently increased the measured score but produced little to no visible perceptual improvement. This suggests that the rationality scorer primarily captures global low-frequency coherence, a property that Stable Diffusion with classifier-free guidance already models effectively.

For LoRA finetuning, optimizing the rationality score alone led to noticeable fading and oversmoothing artifacts. This revealed an important limitation of the reward model: higher naturalness scores do not necessarily correspond to better perceptual image quality.

To address this issue, a differentiable texture penalty was introduced. Although it did not correct semantic distortions or unrealistic object structures, it substantially improved texture preservation and reduced fading artifacts by encouraging higher-frequency image details. The resulting images were perceptually sharper and visually preferable despite achieving slightly lower naturalness scores.

Overall, the experiments demonstrate that realistic image generation requires balancing:
* **Global semantic coherence**,
* **Local texture fidelity**, and
* **Human perceptual quality**.

The findings suggest that future methods should combine global realism objectives with localized and perceptually aligned guidance mechanisms rather than relying solely on image-level naturalness scores.

---

# Key Takeaways

* **Rationality guidance** consistently improves the learned realism score but produces negligible visible improvement.
* **Global naturalness scores** are not reliable proxies for perceptual image quality and can encourage oversmoothing and fading.
* **A differentiable texture penalty** significantly improves texture preservation and image sharpness, demonstrating that reward-model biases can be mitigated through perceptually motivated regularization.
* **The best visual results** are obtained by balancing realism scores with texture preservation rather than maximizing the score alone.
* **Improving image realism** likely requires local guidance mechanisms and reward functions that correlate more strongly with human perception.

> **Main finding:** Higher naturalness scores do not necessarily imply better-looking images. However, incorporating perceptual priors such as texture preservation can meaningfully improve visual quality even when the numerical score decreases.
